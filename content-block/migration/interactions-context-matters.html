One of the most exciting implications of big data for science is that it has the potential to identify complex real world interactions that cannot be found through small studies.

There is a growing body of evidence across many scientific domains, including medicine, agriculture and more that while traditional research approaches are extremely successful within the context of the study, more data is often needed to identify changes in the results across contexts. In health this demonstrates itself through recognition that trial participants need to be more diverse than previously understood as both gender and race can interact with symptoms and effectiveness. In agriculture there is increasing recognition of the importance of interactions with both environmental and cultural factors.

As data scientists working with bigger data we are well placed to be able to identify contextual differences by studying the interactions between variables. The nature of data available to us often means that there are multiple plausible interpretations to interactions observed which the data cannot differentiate between. This is where working collaboratively with people who understand the contexts deeply is valuable to guide decisions related to the data.

The scientific process is about generalising things, and some limitations to this approach have been because traditional methods are in within a context. But the big data approaches we have now are across multiple contexts - we can have more complexity now.
