During the Covid-19 pandemic in 2020, all GCSE and A-Level examinations were cancelled in the UK. Ofqual consequently created a machine learning algorithm to distribute the grades to all students. This video highlights a key concept on variable definitions when using data.

In 2020, all GCSE and A-Level examinations in England and Wales were cancelled due to the Covid-19 pandemic. As a result, Ofqual was tasked with creating an algorithm to predict GCSE and A-Level grades for students. The approach they used to achieve this is outlined in more detail here.

Several factors were used to help build the model. The one we are focussing on here is the current students' performance in previous qualifications. The students’ performance in previous qualifications is referred to as their “prior attainment”. For GCSE level, the prior attainment was taken from the individual’s examination results at the end of Key Stage Two, called SATs. For A-Level students, the prior attainment was from the students’ performance in their GCSEs.

However, in the UK there are multiple GCSE examination boards that schools choose from. Further, GCSEs and IGCSEs, which some schools prefer, are not fully aligned in specifications and content. Hence, the GCSEs variable has multiple definitions behind it. In the Ofqual algorithm documentation openly available there is no mention of any consideration taken to different examination boards, or differentiation between GCSEs and IGCSEs for the purpose of prior attainment.

Given the differences between the definitions in this variable, that could give rise to hidden biases due to merging these potentially different measurements. If privileged or underprivileged schools consistently made different choices, it could lead to unintentional systematic biases from this merging process, which would be hard to identify in the predictions. For Ofqual there were other reasons that led to the algorithm being dropped, but this is a concrete example of a common problem when working with large datasets.

When merging variables from different sources, data analysts should be thoughtful to prevent introducing biases into the data.

This is particularly relevant when a data analyst has multiple data sets that come from different sources. In some instances, it may be sensible to combine, or “merge”, these data sets. This is good practice, and can be very important for the analysis, but needs to be done thoughtfully.

The common pitfall when merging data is that variables can have different definitions when they come from different sources. It is important to check the definition behind the individual variables that you want to combine before proceeding with analysis, as well as how the variable was collected. If the variable definitions and collection process are consistent, then the analysis can continue as usual. However, if they are not consistent, then the data analyst should be aware of potential bias that can be introduced by merging the variables.

When combining variables from different sources, the data analyst should question whether the variable measures the same thing, and has been calculated or collected in the same way for the different sources. They should make corrections where possible. In addition, suppose that the data analyst finds that the merged variable(s) changes for the different sources of data, and the analyst wishes to compare the merged variable(s) for the different sources, such as region. The analyst should question whether a potential effect has occurred because of a change in definition or collection from the data source, or if the potential effect has occurred because of the source variable, such as region.

As well as questioning and correcting potential biases that can be introduced from inconsistent definitions when combining data sets, we recommend adding a new variable that stores the data source, and documenting the differences in associated metadata. Then, at least, no information is lost from working on a merged variable obtained from different definitions.

Measuring Deaths from Snakebite Worldwide

This video gives a small trailor to the example used in this section on a Snakebite, which has recently been recognised by the WHO as a Neglected Tropical Disease. This example is used to illustrate how different variable definitions can occur.

Snakebite has recently been recognised by the WHO as a Neglected Tropical Disease. An apparently straightforward question to ask is: worldwide, how many people per year die from snakebite?

The numbers of reported deaths per year is available from all affected countries, but different countries use different reporting methods: some maintain central records at national or regional level; other rely on hospital presentations; still others use sample surveys.

Simply adding up the numbers could be very misleading. Deaths can be unreported, and the level of under-reporting is likely to vary with the reporting method.

How many Covid-19 Cases?

The Covid-19 pandemic that burst upon the world in 2020 has generated enormous amounts of data, much of which is readily accessible from web-sites that collate data from most, if not all, countries.

As I write this (on 9 December 2022), one site tells me that the worldwide number of cases to date is 652,454,528. And if I dig a bit deeper I find, for example, that for the UK, the number is 24,024,746, slightly fewer than Italy’s 24,488,080.

The corresponding numbers per 100,000 population are approximately 35 and 40 thousand, respectively.

Another site, five minutes later, gives me a worldwide number 648,053,749, UK and Italy numbers 24,281,031 and 24,488,080.

It’s probably not worth agonising too much about the discrepancies between the numbers reported in these two sites. A more important question is: how do you count the number of cases of a disease whose symptoms can vary from mild to lethal?

I don’t know how they count Covid cases in Italy, but in the UK the headline total is a sum of numbers reported from several sources. Two government sources are so-called Pillar 1 and Pillar 2 data, defined as follows:

  • Pillar 1: “all swab tests performed in Public Health England labs and National Health Service hospitals for those with a clinical need, and health and care workers”
  • Pillar 2: “swab testing for the wider population” (Department of Health and Social Care UK, 2020)

It will be immediately apparent to the reader that testing the population at large (Pillar 2) will likely detect mild cases that would not fall within the reach of Pillar 2. And even within Pillar 2, the underlying disease rates in hospitalised patients are likely to be different from the rates in health and care workers. In short, the reported number of cases is a biased estimate of the actual number, and it is not immediately clear how we would quantify, and thus be able to correct for, this bias.

The statistically principled answer is to augment the Pillars 1 and 2 data with data from a randomised survey of the complete population, which will deliver an unbiased estimate but, for reasons of affordability, will likely be conducted on a much smaller scale than Pillars 1 and 2 testing.

Two such surveys were conducted in the UK, the REACT survey (REACT Study Investigators, 2020), which operated from May 2020 to March 2022, and the continuing Office for National Statistics Coronavirus Infection Survey (ONS, 2022). By combining the biased and unbiased data within a single statistical model in which the randomised survey data “anchor” the Pillars 1 and 2 data, we can estimate and adjust for the biases in Pillars 1 and 2 data, and get the best of both worlds: an unbiased estimate with higher precision than would be obtained from the randomised survey data alone; Nicholson et al (2022) show how this was done.

As a practical exercise, have a look where your country is in the league tables on one of the websites above. At time of writing (December 2022), the total number of cases in Britain is amongst the top ten of all countries and regions considered. Similarly so for the total number of deaths in the UK.

However, then look at the data by “death rate per million”. The UK is no longer in the top ten of all countries. At time of writing, they are 25th. In fact, the countries with higher death rates are often very small countries/regions. So, what is going on? Is it that Covid-19 is defined differently in these areas? Is it that Covid-19 is detected differently there?

By comparing rows, we are comparing countries and/or regions to one another. However, there are different mechanisms to detect Covid-19, as well as define Covid-19, depending on the country. In addition, some countries may give their Covid-19 statistics more for political purposes than statistical.

However, this doesn’t stop making the comparison, and does not mean that this data is not useful to compare worldwide comparisons. It is important for the data scientist to take the results with a pinch of salt, and to question the data before making conclusions from it. If we wish to compare two countries, we can drill down on the details of these countries a little further. When drilling down, can we learn why some countries are displaying higher counts than others? What can we learn about the definition of each country? How accessible is that countries testing system? In the UK, testing was not available to the public until months after the pandemic began; however, in other countries the testing was available immediately. A spike in the number of cases in the UK therefore may not necessarily signify a new outbreak, but instead that testing was more widespread.

In addition, smaller countries tend to have a higher death rate per million. Is the information behind this available? Can we think about why this is?
