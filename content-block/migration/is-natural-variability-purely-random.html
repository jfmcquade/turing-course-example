In the Ofqual example, there's there's variability at multiple levels: You have variability at the school level as well as at the individual student level. The Ofqual example is a wonderful case where it is clear that the natural variability is living at different levels. You've got individual variability on how individuals perform. This is something where when we're looking at data, we're almost always trying to understand the natural variability. But that natural variability could be different for different cases or parts of the data. This is one of the difficult aspects: bringing in hierarchical modelling, looking at modelling where there are differences between variability between one case and another case. This is one of the areas where we think about our models differently Natural variability is not random noise, but here it is structured noise. the Natural variability has structure to it.

For any analysis the question of whether the natural variability related to the problem is purely random or has structure should be considered early on. If important structure in the natural variability is not considered the associated variability will remain unidentified.

Hierarchical models capture elements of structure by identifying that natural variability occurs at different ‘levels’. Variables corresponding to these levels are often fitted in the model as "random effects" rather than "fixed effects".

There are also cases where certain subsets of the data have substantially different variability. This might be for a variety of reasons such that a subset is more homogeneous in some unidentified way. Another common reason with merged datasets could be related to different measurement methodologies. Whatever the sources of these differences, the consequence is the same, the analysis can be derailed if they are not identified.

Large data inevitably contains hidden complexity which can at times derail analysis, through the law of large numbers. Data scientists can often gain deep insights by analysing strategic targeted subsets and comparing the natural variability observed in each case. Insights gained by breaking the data down in this way can often be fed back into a full analysis.

This video gives a small trailor to the example used in this section on a placebo-controlled trial of drug treatments for schizophrenia. This example is used to illustrate structure in natural variability.

Background

In this trial, patients with severe schizophrenia were randomised to receive either an active treatment or a placebo. They were followed up for up to 8 weeks.

At weeks 0, 1, 2, 4, 6 and 8 weeks post-randomisation, each patient’s mental state was assessed using a validated questionnaire instrument the Positive and Negative Symptom Score (PANSS). The objective of the trial was to assess which, if either, of the active treatments could reduce the average value of PANSS by at least 20%.

Figure 1 shows a data from six individuals in the randomised trial. Time is on the X-axis, and the measure of the patients symptom severity is on the Y-axis. On the Y-axis, a lower score is good. There are two sets of patients. The patients who received the active treatment are displayed with the dotted lines, and the patients on the placebo are displayed with a solid line.

Figure 1: How the symptoms change for six patients over the course of eight weeks. The patients who received the active treatment are displayed with the dotted lines, and the patients on the placebo are displayed with a solid line.

Random Variability

We want to understand the variability in this data. The first distinction to make is that between random (natural, "bad") variation and systematic (accounted, "good") variation. We can see accountable variation between the two treatments: The solid lines are generally higher than the dotted lines implying that the patients on the active treatment are resulting in less severe symptoms than those on the placebo treatment. This is good: We would hope that the active treatment would reduce symptoms.

There is also some random variability here, which displays quite a bit of structure. We can peel off the different layers to this random variation. A good start is to look at the variation between patients on the same treatment, so we can ignore the systematic variation.

Of the three patients who are on the active treatment, one consistently gives higher scores than the other two, despite being on the same treatment. This implies some variability between the patients which is sustained over the eight weeks of follow up.

In addition, the jagged lines suggest that there's some random variation in what you actually measure at each time point. It seems rather odd to imagine that all of the individual peaks and troughs in these lines are real changes in the patients symptom severity. They're just chance occurrences of the particular exact time at which that measurement was taken.

Another, potentially more subtle, element of variability in this data is that one of the six patients only provided data for the first four weeks. It is not unreasonable to assume, and is true in this case, that the patient stopped providing data because they dropped out of the study. And this drop out was not unrelated to the medical condition that the trial was intending to study (more on this is discussed in the Missing Data content block).

To best analyse this data, it is important to understand the different layers of random variation in the data, and that it is not just random noise.
